The Story: From Inclusive Growth to Concentrated Prosperity

Act I: The UPA Years (2004-2014)

The UPA period wasn't perfect, but it represented a different model. Despite facing the 2008 global financial crisis, India maintained 7.7% average growth while expanding social protection. MGNREGA guaranteed 100 days of employment to rural households. The Right to Information Act empowered citizens. The Right to Education made schooling a fundamental right.

Crucially, employment grew alongside GDP. Between 2004 and 2009, formal sector jobs expanded. Manufacturing employment rose from 11% to 13% of the workforce. While inequality was high (top 1% held 15% of income), it remained stable rather than exploding.

States retained fiscal autonomy. The 13th Finance Commission increased devolution. Special category states received targeted support. There were no cesses eating into the divisible pool. Democratic institutions functioned: press freedom ranked 105th globally in 2004, deteriorating to 140th by 2014 but remaining "Free" by international standards.

Key UPA Achievements:

  • 7.7% growth WITH job creation
  • MGNREGA, RTI, RTE enacted
  • Inequality high but stable
  • Federal balance maintained
  • Democratic institutions respected

Act II: The 2014 Transformation

In 2014, a new government swept to power promising "acche din" (good days) and faster growth. The initial years seemed promising—GDP growth hit 8%, global investors cheered, and ambitious programs like Make in India were launched. But beneath the surface, something fundamental was changing.

The first signs appeared in employment data. Despite 8% growth in 2015-16, job creation stalled. Employment elasticity—the jobs created per unit of GDP growth—collapsed to nearly zero. When the 2017-18 labour survey showed record unemployment at 6.1%, it was delayed until after the 2019 elections.

Then came the shocks. Demonetization in November 2016 wiped out 86% of currency overnight, devastating the informal sector where 87% work. Before recovery could begin, GST implementation in 2017 ended state fiscal autonomy while creating compliance nightmares for small businesses.

Act III: The New Model (2017-2025)

By 2017, a new economic model had crystallized—one that could sustain high GDP growth while concentrating its benefits. The mechanism was ingenious: expand welfare transfers for visibility while suppressing data that showed growing inequality.

Welfare programs like PM-Kisan sent ₹6,000 yearly to farmers—less than 10% of average farm income but highly visible through repeated transfers. Ujjwala provided gas connections that many couldn't afford to refill. Every scheme was centrally branded, though states co-financed implementation.

Meanwhile, wealth concentrated dramatically. The top 1% share of income rose from 15% to 22.6%—exceeding the 1939 colonial peak of 20.7%. When the consumption survey showed this reality, it was suppressed. When the census would have revealed demographic shifts, it was postponed indefinitely.

Electoral Bonds legalized opacity in political finance. Between 2018-2024, ₹18,518 crores flowed through this scheme, with over 90% going to the ruling party. Press freedom rank fell to 161st. India was downgraded from "Free" to "Partly Free" to "Electoral Autocracy."

The New Equilibrium:

  • Growth without quality employment
  • Welfare visibility without structural change
  • Colonial-level inequality
  • Fiscal centralization via cesses/GST
  • Systematic data suppression
  • Democratic institutions hollowed out

Key Findings: Evidence of Transformation

Finding 1: The Jobs Disappeared, Then Returned as Gig Work

The Evidence

Employment tells two different stories. Under UPA, employment elasticity was 0.2—every 1% GDP growth created 0.2% more jobs, mostly formal. Under early NDA (2014-2017), this crashed to 0.01—virtually jobless growth. Then suddenly after 2017, elasticity jumped to 1.11. What happened?

The answer lies in the TYPE of employment. The new jobs were overwhelmingly informal: gig work, platform delivery, contract labor, and self-employment. By 2024, 89% of all employment was informal, up from 82% in 2014. Formal sector employment actually SHRANK from 18% to 11%.

What this means: The unemployment rate falling after COVID masks a harsh reality—people didn't get jobs back, they got gigs. No security, no benefits, no future.

Finding 2: Inequality Exceeded Colonial Peaks

The Numbers Don't Lie

Under British rule in 1939, the richest 1% controlled 20.7% of income—a shameful peak of colonial extraction. After independence, this fell to just 6% by 1982. Under liberalization, it rose to 15% by 2014. But under NDA, it exploded to 22.6% by 2023—exceeding the colonial peak.

Wealth concentration is even worse: the top 1% now own 40.1% of all wealth. That's 166 billionaires controlling two-fifths of national wealth while the bottom 50% own just 3%.

This happened through policy choices: corporate tax cuts from 30% to 22%, no wealth tax, stock market boom benefiting the elite, while demonetization and GST crushed small businesses.

Finding 3: States Lost Fiscal Freedom

The Constitution mandates that taxes be shared between Centre and states. The 14th Finance Commission even raised states' share to 42%. But this was a cruel illusion. Through three mechanisms, states actually LOST revenue:

Cess & Surcharge

10.4% → 20.2%

These special taxes aren't shared with states. They doubled under NDA.

Actual Devolution

42% → 29.8%

Despite constitutional promise, actual transfers fell dramatically.

Annual Loss

Money states should have received but didn't.

Finding 4: When Data Became Political

What Was Hidden

  • ❌ 2017-18 Consumption Survey: Showed first decline in consumption in 40 years. Never released.
  • ❌ 2021 Census: First peacetime postponement in 150 years. Still not conducted.
  • ❌ Employment Data: Delayed until after 2019 elections when showed record unemployment.
  • ❌ GDP Methodology: Changed in 2015, retroactively inflating growth rates.

Who Resigned in Protest

  • • P.C. Mohanan - Acting Chairman, NSC (2019)
  • • J.V. Meenakshi - Member, NSC (2019)
  • • Multiple senior statisticians at NSO
  • • Chief Economic Advisor questioned GDP data

Why this matters: Without reliable data, citizens can't evaluate government claims. Policy is made blind. Democracy requires transparency.

Methods: How We Measured the Unmeasurable

The Challenge

How do you study an economy when the government hides the data? When the 2017-18 consumption survey showed rising poverty, it was suppressed. When unemployment hit record highs, the data was delayed. When the census would reveal uncomfortable truths, it was postponed indefinitely. We had to get creative.

Our Three-Pronged Approach

1. Triangulation Across Sources

We couldn't rely on any single data source, so we combined multiple ones to cross-verify patterns:

  • Government data: Whatever was released, even if delayed
  • Leaked documents: The suppressed consumption survey that journalists obtained
  • International assessments: V-Dem, Freedom House, World Inequality Database
  • Private surveys: CMIE employment data when government data was withheld
  • Corporate filings: Listed company data to verify GDP claims

2. Creating Novel Indices

We developed three new measures to quantify what was happening:

Statistical Suppression Index (SSI)

Formula: SSI = Average(Severity × Salience)

  • • Severity: Suppressed=1.0, Delayed=0.5, Revised=0.3
  • • Salience: Census=1.0, Consumption=0.8, Employment=0.7
Fiscal Centralisation Index (FCI)

Formula: FCI = (Cess_norm + Devolution_inverse + Borrowing_conditions)/3

Captures how fiscal power shifted from states to Centre

Democratic Quality Index (DQI)

Formula: DQI = ∛(Freedom House × Press Freedom × V-Dem)

Geometric mean ensures weakness in any dimension affects overall score

3. Historical Comparison

To understand if changes were NDA-specific or continuing trends, we collected data back to 2004:

  • UPA-I (2004-2009): The high growth phase before global crisis
  • UPA-II (2009-2014): Managing crisis and recovery
  • Modi-I (2014-2019): The transformation begins
  • Modi-II (2019-2024): The new model consolidated

This revealed that many problems (inequality, informalization) existed under UPA but accelerated dramatically under NDA. Others (data suppression, fiscal centralization) were entirely new.

What Now? Three Possible Futures

Scenario 1: Continue Current Path

If Nothing Changes by 2030:

  • • Top 1% will control 30% of income, 50% of wealth
  • • 95% of employment will be informal/gig
  • • States will be fiscally powerless
  • • No reliable economic data will exist
  • • India will be fully autocratic

Economic Consequences:

  • • Consumption will collapse from inequality
  • • Investment will flee uncertainty
  • • Federal conflicts will paralyze governance
  • • Social unrest becomes inevitable
  • • Growth will stagnate below 4%

Historical parallel: Latin America's "lost decade" of the 1980s—high inequality led to economic stagnation, political instability, and social breakdown.

Scenario 2: Gradual Reform

Electoral pressure and economic necessity force partial reforms:

Immediate (6 months):

  • Release suppressed data and conduct census
  • Cap cesses at 10% of gross taxes
  • Restore some press freedom

Medium-term (2 years):

  • Grant statistical agencies independence
  • Create social insurance for gig workers
  • Increase health spending to 2.5% GDP

Outcomes by 2030:

  • Inequality stabilizes but remains high
  • Some formal jobs return
  • Democratic backsliding slows
  • Growth maintains at 5-6%

Scenario 3: Return to Inclusive Growth

A new government or transformed politics enables fundamental reform:

Economic

  • • Wealth tax on top 1%
  • • Universal basic services
  • • Formal job creation focus
  • • 6% GDP on education
  • • 3% GDP on health

Federal

  • • Restore 42% devolution
  • • End conditional transfers
  • • GST Council reform
  • • State fiscal autonomy
  • • Regional development

Democratic

  • • Media ownership caps
  • • Campaign finance reform
  • • Judicial independence
  • • RTI strengthening
  • • Civil society freedom

Historical parallel: South Korea's democratic transition (1987-1997) combined political liberalization with policies that reduced inequality and created a broad middle class.

The Choice Is Ours

India stands at a crossroads. We can continue concentrating wealth and power while most Indians struggle. We can make partial reforms that slow but don't reverse decline. Or we can choose inclusive growth that benefits all.

Democracy is not a spectator sport. The future depends on what we demand today.
