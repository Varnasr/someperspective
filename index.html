Some Perspective: India's Economic Reality

Welcome to "Some Perspective"

This interactive website presents comprehensive research on India's economic transformation from 2004 to 2025, examining how two distinct political periods—the UPA (2004-2014) and NDA (2014-2025)—produced fundamentally different models of growth, governance, and democratic accountability.

Why This Research Matters

India stands at a critical juncture. While official narratives celebrate high GDP growth and global rankings, the reality for most Indians tells a different story. This research provides evidence-based analysis showing how:

  • 📊 Growth without jobs: GDP rises while formal employment falls to historic lows
  • 💰 Colonial-era inequality: Top 1% income share exceeds 1939 levels
  • 🏛️ Fiscal centralization: States lose autonomy through design, not accident
  • 📈 Statistical suppression: Key data withheld when it contradicts narratives
  • 🗳️ Democratic erosion: Institutions weakened while maintaining electoral competition

Executive Summary

India's political economy underwent fundamental transformation between 2014-2025, characterized by high GDP growth alongside democratic erosion, creating a new governance model: "growth without accountability."

Five Core Findings

1. Jobless Growth Accelerated

Despite 6.2% average GDP growth (2014-2025), formal employment fell from 17.5% to 10.8%. Youth unemployment peaked at 23% in 2025, while women's workforce participation stagnated at 24%.

2. Inequality Reached Colonial Peaks

Top 1% income share rose from 15.6% (2014) to 23.4% (2025), exceeding the 1939 colonial peak of 20.7%. Bottom 50% share fell to 12.8%, the lowest in recorded Indian history.

3. Fiscal Architecture Centralized

States' share in taxes fell from 42% to 38.5%, while cess/surcharge (not shared) rose from 10.4% to 21.2% of gross tax revenue. GST compensation ended without replacement.

4. Statistical System Compromised

2017-18 consumption survey suppressed, 2021 census indefinitely postponed, employment data delayed by 2+ years. Statistical Suppression Index rose from 2.3 to 8.1 (scale 0-10).

5. Democratic Quality Declined 44%

V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index fell from 0.71 to 0.40. Press freedom rank dropped to 161/180. Internet shutdowns increased 2,000% from 2014 levels.

Three Novel Indices: Measuring Institutional Erosion

To systematically track India's institutional trajectory, this research develops three composite indices that capture different dimensions of democratic erosion and governance quality.

📊 Statistical Suppression Index (SSI)

Measures state interference in production and dissemination of official statistics.

Components:

  • • Delayed/suppressed surveys
  • • Statistical office resignations
  • • Methodological discontinuities
  • • Census postponements

Key Findings:

  • • 2014: 2.3 (Low suppression)
  • • 2020: 10.0 (Peak during COVID)
  • • 2025: 8.1 (High suppression)
  • • 252% increase overall

💰 Fiscal Centralization Index (FCI)

Tracks erosion of fiscal federalism and state autonomy.

Components:

  • • Cess/surcharge share
  • • States' tax share decline
  • • Conditional transfers rise
  • • Borrowing restrictions

Key Findings:

  • • 2014: 0.62 (Moderate)
  • • 2020: 0.84 (High during COVID)
  • • 2025: 0.79 (High centralization)
  • • 27% increase overall

🗳️ Democratic Quality Index (DQI)

Composite measure of democratic institutions and practices.

Components:

  • • V-Dem scores
  • • Press freedom rankings
  • • Civil society space
  • • Electoral integrity

Key Findings:

  • • 2014: 0.71 (Moderate quality)
  • • 2020: 0.48 (Low quality)
  • • 2025: 0.40 (Very low)
  • • 44% decline overall

Analytical Framework

1. Comparative Period Analysis

Systematic comparison of UPA (2004-2014) and NDA (2014-2025) periods across 50+ indicators. Controls for global economic conditions, commodity cycles, and external shocks.

2. State-Level Panel Data

Analysis of 28 states with fixed effects models to identify policy impacts while controlling for state-specific characteristics and time trends.

3. Triangulation Strategy

Cross-verification using multiple independent sources. When official data is suppressed, uses proxy indicators and international assessments.

Limitations & Caveats

Important Limitations

  • • Census Gap: No census since 2011 limits demographic analysis
  • • Consumption Data: 2017-18 survey suppressed; using proxy estimates
  • • State Variation: National aggregates mask significant state-level differences
  • • Informal Economy: Measurement challenges persist despite methodology improvements
  • • COVID Impact: Pandemic effects complicate 2020-2021 comparisons

Implications: What This Means for India

Policy Recommendations

1. Restore Statistical Independence

  • • Grant constitutional status to National Statistical Commission
  • • Mandatory release timelines for all surveys
  • • Independent audit of methodology changes
  • • Conduct Census 2021 immediately with caste enumeration

2. Rebalance Fiscal Federalism

  • • Constitutional cap on cess/surcharge at 10% of gross tax
  • • Restore states' share to 42% as per 14th Finance Commission
  • • Remove conditionalities on state borrowing
  • • Create GST Compensation Fund 2.0

3. Strengthen Democratic Institutions

  • • Electoral finance transparency (post-Electoral Bonds)
  • • Protect press freedom through independent media council
  • • End internet shutdowns except in extreme emergencies
  • • Restore FCRA licenses for legitimate NGOs

4. Address Employment Crisis

  • • National Employment Guarantee Act for urban areas
  • • Manufacturing focus with 25% job reservation
  • • Formalization incentives for MSMEs
  • • Skills training aligned with Industry 4.0

The Choice Ahead

India faces a critical choice: continue on the path of centralized growth without accountability, or rebuild the institutional foundations of inclusive democracy. The evidence shows current trajectories are unsustainable. Reform is not just desirable—it's essential for India's future.
